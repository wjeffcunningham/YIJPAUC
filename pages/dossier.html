#01. Hired illegally (without permits)

In the 3rd draft of “Twilight Zone—Landis Episode,” dated June 13th, 1982, a new Vietnamese Village sequence was added (Scenes 28-32) in which the anti-hero protagonist redeems himself by rescuing two orphans from a marauding combat helicopter. Several of the filmmakers, in an effort to maximize realism despite labor laws, conspired to cast the parts illegally (without permits). The conspiracy would spiral to include several lower-ranked members of the production, forcing them to risk their careers by speaking out, or else be made complicit.

Folsey later told LaBrecque that drawing others into the illegal hiring is “one of the things I regret most. We put a lot of people on the crew in a very compromising position: Elie Cohn, people in wardrobe, and assistant directors, anybody who had knowledge that the kids were going to be used illegally and had to go along with us, in effect, in breaking the law. That made me very uncomfortable. We did a very bad thing there” (69).
  1. June 13: Third draft; children added to rescue sequence.
  2. June 16: Casting session; casting agent raises legal and safety concerns.

    Associate casting agent, Marci Liroff, of Fenton & Fineberg Casting, although just 24-years-old, had already worked with Amblin Entertainment twice before, on Poltergeist (1982) and E.T.: The Extra-Terrestrial (1982).

    On July 7th, 1983, police investigators spoke with Liroff, who said:

    ... on 6/16/82 she had been one of four people at a "Casting Session" which took place in John Landis' office at Universal Studios. Present at the casting session were herself, her boss (Mike Fenton), George Folsey, and John Landis. During this session, Mr. Landis explained to everyone present that the character ("Bill") being portrayed by VICTOR MORROW in the Landis episode was coming across too harsh in the movie. Mr. Landis said that during the previous evening, he had come up with an idea, "softening Morrow's character." Mr. Landis explained that his idea included Vic Morrow running across a river at night being pursued by a helicopter which would be firing simulated machine gunfire. As Mr. Morrow was running across the river, he would be carrying two children. This scene was supposed to depict Vic Morrow rescuing these two Vietnamese children as a Vietnamese Village was under attack. Mr. Landis continued that, as Mr. Morrow crossed the river carrying the two children, numerous large explosions would be occurring to either side and to the front and back of the actors.

    Mr. Landis told Ms. Liroff that they wanted her to obtain two children “Vietnamese, Korean or Thai, approximately 6 or 7 years of age” for use in the aforementioned scene. She stated that based on the children being in close proximity to explosions and close proximity to a helicopter, there was no way she would be involved in hiring the children.

    She also explained that because of the danger, there was no way they could obtain necessary permits through the normal channels. She stated that she continued explaining to Mr. Landis and those present that additionally, the entire scene was to be shot in the late night and early morning hours and for that reason and in addition to the danger, there was no way proper permits could be obtained. Ms. Liroff continued by telling Mr. Landis that she would not be involved with the procurement of the children for this scene. She stated that her boss, Mike Fenton, concurred with her.

    Ms. Liroff continued that John Landis, after receiving her refusal to obtain the children, immediately turned towards George Folsey and began to hold a conversation with him. During this time, Dan Allingham entered the office. Ms. Liroff stated that Landis, Folsey, and Allingham began having a conversation about hiring children in spite of the fact that they couldn't get them through normal legal channels.

    She stated that John Landis told Folsey and Allingham that they would "just have to get the kids off the street, we won't put them on any records and we won't tell anyone what we're doing."

    She recalls that George Folsey commented something to the effect, "Okay fine, that's how we'll do it." (Police report)

    Liroff also supplied the police with her casting notes for the day, where she’d written,

    “Two Vietnamese kids, boy/girl (Chinese or Korean, Thai. Two nights.)”

    Besides substantiating that Landis had asked them to procure children at all (which Landis denied), Liroff’s testimony also established that the filmmakers believed that the shooting would or could take “two nights.”

    This is significant because the parents were only given an abridged version of the sequence before committing to participate, and were told they would only be needed between 7:00pm—1:00am on a single night. But once they arrived on set, the filmmakers casually and repeatedly extended the families’ time commitment across two nights, past the point where all four parents were able to supervise their children as planned, and to the point of exhaustion. Along with the misrepresentation of the danger involved, then, the misrepresentation of the time commitment is a major issue in assessing culpability.

    By 1984, Liroff supplemented her police statement, going on the record about the involvement of Executive Producer Frank Marshall (Raiders of the Lost Ark, Poltergeist, E.T.).

    She testified that two days after the alarming casting session, during a scheduled production phone call with Marshall, whom she knew from her work on Poltergeist and E.T., she alerted him to Landis’s explicitly-stated intentions to hire children “off the street.” He told her he would take care of it.

    During cross-examination, the defense suggested that she couldn't have been seriously alarmed by Landis’s statements, because she hadn't followed-up about the matter further at the time.

    The young casting agent responded, “I told the director it was illegal. I told the producer, the production manager. I didn’t know it was my job to be a policeman. In hindsight I wish I had.” (qtd. in LaBrecque, 162)

  3. June 26: Fourth draft; budget increase approved.
  4. July 6: Discussions about hiring children illegally (without permits).

    The issue of the children lay dormant for the rest of June as the Landis group geared up for production, starting with his prologue to the film, "Real Scary".

    Meanwhile, the new Vietnamese Village sequence's was still awaiting official studio approval, which it received late in the month. Landis's group would receive an additional $200,000 to make it, on top of the segment's existing $1,000,000 budget (Chain Ch. 18). Landis turned in a fourth and final draft on June 26th, 1982, but it contained only a few minor adjustments.

    After the production got notice that the budget had been officially approved by the studio, the account of how the hiring proceeded varies: namely, between that of Co-Producer and Director John Landis, and his "office" co-defendants, and that of Executive Producer Frank Marshall, known only by inference, in the absence of any public reference to the matter except for his 1990 LA Times interview.

    The problem faced by the Landis group was that according to California state labor laws, well-known within the film industry, children under the age of 8 could only be worked until 6:30pm.

    Marshall has never accounted for basic questions concerning his failure to prevent the illegal hiring of the two children for a dangerous practical effects scene despite being alerted to Landis’s intention to do exactly that over a month in advance.

    Defendants Landis, Folsey, and Allingham, for their part, have consistently maintained that after the budget was approved, Marshall twice visited their offices, an assemblage of trailers on the Universal Studios lot, to address the practical issue presented by the addition of the Vietnamese Village rescue sequence to the script: once early in the week of July 5-9th, 1982, and once at the end of that same week (see Braun’s letters to the DA; Landis’s trial attorney James Neal’s opening statement; Landis’s trial testimony; Folsey’s interviews with journalists).

    The discussion the first day, the defendants say, turned on whether it would be possible for the production to get waivers from the labor board that would allow the children to work at night legally.

    According to the defendants, Marshall said he would have “the woman who works with me call the labor board and see about the viability of getting waivers.” (LaBrecque 13.) They assert that Marshall was referring to Associate Producer, Kathleen Kennedy.

  5. July 9: Defendants conspire to hire children illegally.

    On Friday the 9th, the defendants say, Marshall returned to the offices and informed them that the call had been made, and that unfortunately waivers could only be gotten until 8:00 or 8:30pm. However, this being July, it wouldn’t even be dark by 8:00 or 8:30pm.

    So that the children could work despite the late hour, the co-defendants decided to go ahead with Landis’s plan to hire children which—they say—happened with Marshall’s explicit support.

    Landis testified about the meeting as follows:

    JL: Frank told us that they had called the labour board and the labour board said they would give a waiver but only until eight or eight-thirty, which to our purposes then, our state of mind then, wouldn't accomplish what we needed because it was still light at eight-thirty."

    James Neal (Landis’s trial attorney): Was any decision made at this time regarding the use of children?"

    JL: Yes.

    Neal: What was that decision, John?

    JL: We decided to break the law. We decided, wrongly, to violate the labor code.

    Neal: John, what was your thinking at that time?"

    JL: I thought, and we discussed, that we would honor not the letter of the law but the spirit of the law. And I thought, and we discussed, that we would find children whose parents—we would explain to them that we were doing a technical violation, that we were working them without a teacher on the set—explain to them what we were going to do. And if we could find people who would fulfill that function as we thought, and I can only speak for my state of mind then, and I understand that was wrong—but we decided to go ahead and try to find parents who would fulfill that, what I thought would be the spirit of the law, and be the guardians, be there with them on the set.

    Neal: John, that was wrong. You recognize that?

    JL: Yes.

    Neal: Out of this meeting or as a result of this meeting, John, was anybody given the task of obtaining the children?

    JL: Frank [Marshall] and George [Folsey, Jr.] volunteered to try and find people.

    In the end, they say, the task fell to Folsey.

    Folsey later told LaBrecque:

    We wouldn’t have hired the kids on a Spielberg picture unless either Spielberg or his people knew it. I mean, that would have been a terrible thing to do, put them in that position. The fact that Frank [Marshall] agreed to do it made me feel that it was okay, that it was really his responsibility, too. (14)

    And legally speaking, Folsey was correct—a point confirmed by Allingham’s attorney in 1985. According to standards set by the Division of Labor Standards Enforcement (DLSE), the production company was deemed the sole employer of minors and crew members, with liability for their employment and supervision falling squarely on the company’s officers.

    By July 9th, Folsey only had 12 days until the last scheduled day of shooting, July 21st, and scrambled for solutions.

    After the 3rd draft had been typed back in June, Landis ordered his studio-assigned typist (Alpha Campbell) to keep it under lock and key. This created an operational bottleneck as various production personnel attempted to get a copy of the latest draft from the production coordinator (Donna Schuman) who herself was being denied by Campbell.

    Schuman asked Folsey (a family friend outside of work) about the reason for the secrecy, and he told her:

    “Because John has written two children into the script. We’re going to have to work them without permits. And we don’t want that information getting out.” (qtd. in F&G, 70)

    The conspiracy soon expanded to include a production secretary (Cynthia Nigh); the 1st AD (Elie Cohn); the 2nd AD (Anderson House); and a DGA intern (Hilary Leach, acting mostly as a PA).

    House clocked the violation as soon as he was brought on board and read the script:

    “I assumed that there was stage work and that we’d be working with the children on a stage so that the children would not be working after hours—later than 6:30. He [Unit Production Manager Dan Allingham] responded that, no, they were going to be working “night for night.” (Grand jury)

    In Leach’s words, “as a member... of the DGA we know the rules, I know the rules, and I knew that we were not following the rules.” (Grand jury)

    However, comprehension of the seriousness of the action they were entering into varied, with many unaware of how Landis actually intended to shoot the scene. Perhaps he had devised a way to do so safely.

    Schuman assumed that the children would obviously not be placed in any proximity to the conflagration Landis had been hyping-up around the office for weeks.

    It was always described as a big, huge explosion. John said he was going to blow that village off the face of the earth. You couldn’t put two little kids in the middle of that. (qtd. in F&G, 82)

    At Folsey’s request, Schuman even volunteered her husband (Dr. Hal Schuman) as a pipeline to LA’s Asian community. He had previously managed a community mental health center predominantly staffed by and serving Asian clientele, and remained friendly with several of the counsellors still working there.

  6. July 12-14: Children solicited.

    Dr. Schuman called Dr. Peter Chen. He kept it brief, explaining that his wife worked for Warner Brothers, and that they were looking for two Asian children, a boy and a girl, ages 6 to 8, for a few face close-ups, and to be carried across a river by an actor (Dr. Chen to the NTSB). He was calling to ask if Dr. Chen knew of any suitable families that might be interested in participating.

    (Dr. Schuman may or may not have mentioned special effects and a lack of permitting—on this point Dr. Schuman and Dr. Chen disagreed.)

    Dr. Chen thought about his own 6-year-old niece, Renee, and referred Dr. Schuman to his colleague, Dr. Daniel Le, a Vietnamese refugee-immigrant himself, who had two sons, 4-year-old Christopher, and 7-year-old My-ca (“America-Canada,” after the Les asylum nations).

    Dr. Chen relayed the opportunity to his brother and Dr. Le, respectively, who each presented it to their children, who were thrilled by the prospect. Each family confirmed their interest with Dr. Schuman, who then relayed an invitation to visit the set to meet the producers (Landis and Folsey), who would give them more details and answer their questions. The studio would send a van to take them there and back, northwest through Los Angeles, two days later on Friday, July 16th.

    Meanwhile, Folsey’s search had taken him to Elysian Heights elementary school in a then-predominantly Asian neighborhood (Echo Park) where he was put in touch with an educational support worker, Ms. Julie Hua, and her sister who, between them, had six children and a niece suitable for the roles. They were invited to the set the following night, Thursday, July 15th—the evening before the Chen and Le families were set to arrive.

  7. July 15-16: Casting sessions at Franklin Canyon.

    Folsey picked up the Hua sisters and their seven children in a limousine (Chain 133), and accompanied them across town to Franklin Canyon Park, near to Warner Bros. and Universal Studios. The bona fide Hollywood movie set was buzzing with about a hundred cast, crew, and guests passing through, filming the 1954 Alabama KKK timeline. Landis looked over the children, and selected the two youngest for the roles.

    On Friday, July 16th, the Chen and Le families were picked up by a driver in a white van and driven across the city to Franklin Canyon Reservoir for their meeting with Landis and Folsey. Only Dr. Le was absent among the four parents, as he was assured there was no need for him (or his wife for that matter) to miss their usual Friday night professional development workshop.

    About 15-20 minutes after the families arrived, Folsey approached, along with John Landis’s mother (Shirley Levine). Introductions were made, and then, as they waited for Landis to become available, Folsey summarized the sequence:

    Hoa-Kim Le (HKL): He showed me who is Vic Morrow, I never met him before. And, "that man is Vic Morrow" and he said, "Vic Morrow walk up in village and he saw only two Vietnamese children, one boy and one girl, and they are the only two survivors in the village and then we pretend like the helicopter come over and shoot at the village. Shoot and bomb the village." And in my mind, I thought he, the village was bombed. Oh, yes, he told me, "The village was bombed, was destroyed and there are only two children, two survivors from the village. So, and then after that, the helicopter was come out, and Vic Morrow just take the children and run across the river."

    Sheriff’s Department (SD): That was after the village was bombed?

    HKL: Right. After the village was bombed. Because the children are the only two survivors in the village after the bomb.

    SD: This was who telling you this again?

    HKL: George Folsey, the producer, co-producer. That’s why he told me only two survivors in the village. And then, he said "Vic Morrow will walk across the river and we put a little," ah, just like he said, "a little things on the river bed and something — Like a firecracker sound. ... it must be a little scary but it's very safe.”

    The Chens remembered Folsey's summary in less detail.

    NTSB: So as far as you knew, the children were only going [to be] carried across the river?

    Mark Chen: Carried across the river. That’s all. That’s why he said the children didn’t have to speak anything. The actor just carries the two children across the river.

    Only Hoa-Kim Le recounted an ending in which, after Vic and the children reached the safety of a shed across the river, a helicopter was to pick them up.

    Folsey maintains that he did tell the families about the explosives, which is consistent with the accounts of the production secretaries (Schuman and Nigh) who overheard Folsey mentioning the explosives in broken English over the phone as he described the scene to parents of prospective actors, although downplaying the pyrotechnical aspect of the sequence as “like watching fireworks.”

    Schuman asked Folsey why he bothered to mention the explosives to parents if they would not affect the children. “It is the right thing to do,” she quoted him as saying. “When we are going to have explosions on the set, you ought to tell everybody up front. I mean, you know, G_d forbid anything goes wrong, and then if you tell everybody then you are covered.” (qtd. in LaBrecque, 21)

    Finally Landis arrived with Morrow in tow; more introductions were made, and then Landis told Folsey to hire them.

    Folsey broke the news to Mrs. Hua the following day, that her sister’s two children were no longer being considered for the roles.

    Mr. Folsey said in a very low and sorry voice that our children had not been chosen... He said Mr. Landis had found children younger and smaller. We were very disappointed. (qtd. in F&G, 116)

    He’d already promised the Huas some money for their trouble, however, and said he would drop it off that Wednesday evening (July 21st) along with an additional $300, if she and her family could stay on standby until 9:00pm in case the two smaller children couldn’t act.

  8. July 19: The production moves to Indian Dunes.

    The production migrated to Indian Dunes on Monday, July 19th.

    That evening, according to the 1st AD (Elie Cohn), he, Landis, Folsey, and Allingham held a clandestine meeting to discuss “what day to bring the children, and to make it as short as possible.”

  9. July 20: First day of filming at Indiana Dunes; live ammunition used on set.

    On the morning of Tuesday, July 20th, a 75-year-old Warner Brothers accountant (Jim Henderling) received a call from Bonne Radford.

    Radford is listed in the Twilight Zone production directory as an “accountant” from the “Warner Bros” faction of the production. Radford is the longtime paymaster/accountant of Amblin Entertainment, and sister of Mary Radford, who is listed as the “Ass’t to Frank Marshall”.

    Pursuant to the deal memo negotiated between Spielberg, Landis, Marshall, and Warner Bros., all production expenses above $500 needed to be co-signed by two gatekeepers: Henderling, a 75-year-old payroll watchdog on the side of the studio and Marshall, as the Executive Producer, on the side of the two Producer/Directors.

    Henderling told investigators that Radford asked him “how they could hire children without going through the Screen Extras Guild.” (Police report)

    He told her that he didn’t know, but would make some inquiries. He called the Screen Extras Guild but remained in the dark. He then mentioned the request to his boss, the Warner Bros VP of Production assigned to the film, Ed Morey, who disapproved.

    But before Henderling could get back to Radford, she called him, explaining that they would not be using children in the end after all, so the point was moot. Instead, she asked if Henderling could co-sign a $2,000 cheque payable to George Folsey, Jr., for a “personal cash advance,” that she would courier to Henderling immediately.

    Henderling said he would ask Morey and call her back. He apprised Morey of the situation and that he suspected the production intended to use the money to hire children illegally. “The whole method seemed unorthodox,” Henderling testified; “I didn’t feel like signing it.” (176.)

    But Morey advised him that an Associate Producer had every right to request such an advance, and told Henderling to sign the cheque.

    The cheque, with Henderling’s and Marshall’s signatures on it, became a key piece of evidence linking Twilight Zone Productions, Inc., as well as the production’s payroll entity, DISC Management Services, Inc., to the illegal hiring.

    Meanwhile, the production had begun shooting the story’s Vietnam sequence, in which Morrow’s character emerges from a tropical swamp before hearing a Viet Cong patrol, and hiding himself behind some reeds. But his rustling is noticed by an U.S. Army patrol, who halt, and wait for him to settle, before spraying the plants with machine gun fire.

    During the production of this scene, Morrow (who’d agreed to do most of his own stunts) was waiting behind the plants for Landis’s signal, at which point he would leap out of the way, and then the special effects team would fire with semi-automatic marble guns. But the pressure was weak, and they weren’t punching through the leaves. The Special Effects Coordinator (Paul Stewart) offered to replace them with squibs (small explosives) but when Landis found out that it would take 15 minutes, Stewart suggested they use live ammunition instead; he happened to have three Remington shotguns and ammo in the back of the special effects truck.

    Many of the crew were startled by the practice at the time, even in the context of a shoot already fraught with technical snags, safety violations, and near-misses, and among Landis’s notoriously rough, ramshackle—and loaded—production team.

    Surely Marshall was aware of at least some of these red flags?

    According to Chain, in August 1982, Marshall’s lawyers downplayed his involvement to Cal/OSHA, emphasizing the distinction between Spielberg’s group and Landis’s group, which kept separate offices on separate lots. Marshall was dismissed from consideration for indictment on the basis that he had only been on the set during 3 of the 12 days of production, when in fact it had been at least 8 (Chain, Ch. 25).

  10. July 21–22: First night of filming with the children.

    According to Farber & Green, “As they entered the gates of the park, George Folsey turned to Dr. Le. He was relieved to be on private property... The county officials would not be able to get in.” (118)

    As of Wednesday, July 21st, the Chen and Le families were still under the impression that their obligation to the filmmakers was only for six hours, 7:00pm-1:00am. Mrs. Chen had made clear at the July 16th meeting that she was conscientious about her son's sleep schedule.

    But unbeknownst to them, the schedule the filmmakers had given them was nothing short of highly ambitious: subsequent to their arrival on-set, the production—still bogged down shooting a tracking shot of Morrow walking into the village—required the children to shoot a dramatically significant scene inside the hut. And after that, they still had the full rescue/river-crossing sequence, involving three distinct multi-camera helicopter-and-explosives setups, along with various pick-up shots.

    The famlies, meanwhile, were being sequestered in a trailer a mile away from the set, bewildered by the long delay.

    Mark Chen:

    When we got there, we were waiting until midnight. What we had been told was the hours we would be there was only from 7 to 1 o'clock in the morning. It's over midnight. We said, "How come, there's nothing to do for our children?" So they say, "Well, just be patient, there will be."

    Hoa-Kim Le:

    I keep asking him until now he said "We cannot do it. We have to do it after dinner." I said, "what you mean by dinner? When is dinner? I never have dinner. A man there said twelve o'clock." I said "Midnight? Dinner? This is strange to me and I expect he finish before midnight." but then he said. "We worked day already." We already got there and what can I say, "No I don't want it? I go home?" I'm not that type woman. He said there's no danger. So we stayed there, we stayed in the mobile home.

    The Special Effects Supervisor (Paul Stewart) told LaBrecque, "I knew by one o'clock that we would never finish... When you do a motion picture you know the Assistant Director is gonna say, 'Oh, yeah, we're gonna finish.' But you walk away knowing he's full of it. I know what I gotta do and it's not humanly possible." (30)

    Folsey was advised by 1st AD (Elie Cohn) at 2:00am that it would be impossible to complete everything that had been planned for the night.

    Landis testified that, at 4:30am,

    “Elie came to me and said the dolly shot had taken so long and we were behind, and he felt that once we finished the dialogue scene, he recommended that we send the actors home and not rush anything and just go a day over schedule.”

    “Alright. What did you say about that?”

    “I thought it sounded like a good idea and I went to Frank Marshall and George Folsey and we talked about it and agreed to do it.”

    Folsey informed the parents on the set, provisionally asking them if they would consider returning the following day. He secured confirmation back at the trailer as he was paying them out for their childrens's night’s work.

    NTSB: You were saying that Mr. Folsey was talking to you about that the children just need to show up by themselves, and that the parents, you and your wife, didn't need to come. Is that correct?

    MC (Mark Chen): That's correct.

    NTSB: What was your response to that?

    MC: To be a parent, we really want to be with the children.

    NTSB: I understand.

    MC: It was the first—the face shoot. It looked very simple. You know, they didn't have to talk, they didn't have to open their mouths. They just looked at the actor, Vic Morrow, that's all. When he says it's going to be very quick, they tried to finish earlier, so actually the parents don't have to come, just have the children show up, that's all.

    NTSB: You mean they didn't say anything about any other shot? They were going to re-take the shot, just taking a picture of the children that evening?

    MC: We hadn't seen the actor carry the children across the river. So we know that would be the next shot. After that, that will be completed. They take so long. He says, "Just come back tonight, and they will try to do it as soon as they can."

    NTSB: Have your child back home, is that what you're saying?

    MC: He said he'd try to make it before 3:30 a.m.

    NTSB: 3:30 in the morning? That would be Friday morning?

    MC: Friday morning. Since I was very tired, so I say, “okay.” I told my wife I'd have her come with Renee. The same thing with Le's family. The father was the one to take My-Ca on Thursday.

    The Chen family knew they were committing to another late night, but had no idea that the roll-over day would be of an entirely different tenor, reserved for the segment’s pyrotechnical and helicopter shots.

    The Le family, however, were under the impression they were going to be finished within an hour or two.

#2. Extreme negligence

The difficulty of the Twilight Zone case was that the determination of criminality required more discretionary judgment than in other crimes that resulted in death. Budds told me: “This was a subjective intellectual interpretation of what constitutes reckless and wanton conduct. Murder is murder, because — boom! — you shoot somebody and there’s a bullet hole. In the opinion of my witnesses, who lived through the experience, [the actions of Landis and the others were] reckless and wanton.” (LaBrecque 88)

California Penal Code...

Homicide...

Manslaughter...

...§192. Manslaughter is the unlawful killing of a human being without malice. It is of three kinds:

  1. Voluntary—upon a sudden quarrel or heat of passion.
  2. Involuntary—in the commission of an unlawful act, not amounting to a felony; or in the commission of a lawful act which might produce death, in an unlawful manner, or without due caution and circumspection. This subdivision shall not apply to acts committed in the driving of a vehicle.
  3. Vehicular... [prosecuted as a distinct category, owing to its nuances and larger body of precedent].

This section partially consolidates clear warnings, negligence, and escalation leading to the fatal helicopter crash, supporting the California Penal Code §192 involuntary manslaughter charge.

Landis and his subordinates conceded that they had hired the children illegally (the unlawful act) but not that their actions met the standard of “gross negligence.” That is, a serious deviation from the standard of care expected from an industry professional (or even citizen layperson) in the same situation.

The state concedes that although the defendant(s) didn’t intend the harm done, their actions were still of a criminal character. This is because, if the substantial risk of harm was foreseeable (when measured against the actions of a hypothetical reasonable person acting in their place), and yet the defendant(s) proceeded anyway, they either noticed the danger and behaved extremely recklessly in disregarding it; or, if they didn’t notice the danger, should have if not for some prior act(s) of willful ignorance, or carelessness that predictably rendered them unable to perform their work at the accepted professional standard. The charge only gets stronger as the defendants admit or can be shown to have been conscious of the danger (approaching, finally, “implied malice”).

Although one would expect that the basic elements of the scene (explosives, helicopter, night, water work) would make danger obvious, Landis et al claimed ignorance, citing their confidence in their technicians to get the shot safely: namely, the Stunt Coordinator (Gary McLarty); the SFX Coordinator (Paul Stewart); the pilot (Dorcey Wingo); and six Fire Safety Officers.

#03. Five corporations and nine individuals

On August 3, 1982, within two weeks of the incident in question, a civil action Chen et al. v. Warner Bros. et al. was filed in pursuit of damages estimated at $200 million. The complaint, filed in the Superior Court, named numerous entities—corporate and individual—as defendants. Such breadth in initial filings is consistent with litigation arising from complex incidents involving multiple parties, where ensuring inclusion of all potentially liable contributors is a strategic necessity. (See LaBrecque, p. 68; Farber & Green, p. 177)

The complaint named the following five corporate defendants:
  1. Bell Helicopters, manufacturer of the helicopter involved;
  2. The Burbank Lot, the lessor of land to Warner Bros. Studios;
  3. Newhall Land & Fanning Company, owner and lessor of the Indiana Dunes site;
  4. Warner Bros. Studios, the principal production entity;
  5. Western Helicopters, supplier of the helicopter and pilot in question.
Additionally, nine individuals were named in their professional capacities, as follows:
  1. Dan Allingham, Unit Production Manager;
  2. Joe Dante, Co-director (Segment 3);

#05. A Grand Jury hearing, a disorienting preliminary hearing, and an appeal gone awry

Grand Jury (May/June 1983):

Kesselman conducted a tour-de-force, involving over thirty witnesses, and all five defendants were indicted on all five of the requested counts. Twilight Zone: The Movie (1983) opened on the same day.

Preliminary Hearing (January/February 1984):

At this point, the defense had sharpened their “delamination theory” arguing, effectively, that the helicopter’s tail rotor hadn’t been blown off, per se, because the explosives they were using shouldn’t be that powerful, but rather that its skin had “delaminated” under extreme heat. The defense argued that this so-called “delamination” was unique in aviation history and therefore, necessarily, unforeseeable. Although Judge Crahan was skeptical about the delamination argument, he decided to let the three office defendants off the hook for those counts.

Appeal Hearing (November 1984):

The defense pushed their luck. They gambled on an appeal hearing requesting the case be thrown out altogether. Instead, Judge Ringer re-introduced the two office counts as having been incorrectly dismissed!

Braun sent two letters to the DA intending to point out the unfairness of prosecuting the office counts when several other high level executives could also be presumed to know about the illegal hiring: why weren’t they being prosecuted? It didn't bother these "acknowledged experts" at the time, evidently; ergo, those charges should be “disposed of” against Braun's clients.

It was of no use, however, and the case was off to trial.

Interim (1984-1986):

In the interim, both Landis’s lead counsel, Harland Braun, and the DA’s prosecutor, Gary Kesselman, were replaced. Braun was demoted in favour of Watergate prosecutor, James Neal, but stayed on as Folsey’s lawyer; and Kesselman was taken off the case shortly after a “taxi dance” club he co-owned was raided by police and found to be employing illegal immigrants.

As well, Kesselman was not liked by the incoming DA Ira Reiner. Kesselman, still unsure about the significance of the delamination theory, favoured accepting a plea deal offered by the defense: plead guilty but no jail time. Reiner was more bullish: “What the hell, does anyone care whether it is delamination or debris? You’ve got two dead kids. You’ve got a helicopter. You’ve got explosives. What more do you need? ... Landis is going to go to the state prison on this!” (Chain Ch. 40).

Kesselman was replaced by Lea D’Agostino, who had a successful track record prosecuting career criminals and sex offenders but was inexperienced in the vagaries of “Involuntary.”

#09. You’ll Never Eat Lunch In This Town Again (Index Locorum)

At the 46th Academy Awards, The Sting (1973) celebrated a monster year. They’d been a surprise hit at the box office, stealing The Exorcist’s thunder by opening faster and wider. Then, the word of mouth made the movie into an experience, and had friends and families coming back all summer long.

Julia Phillips became the first woman to win an Oscar for Best Picture until Wendy Finerman became the second in 1994, with Forrest Gump.

Phillips would go on to produce—alongside her ex-husband, Michael Phillips—Taxi Driver and Close Encounters, two of the defining movies of the late '70s. While her ex-husband took the lead on Taxi Driver, Phillips took the lead on Close Encounters.

At the same time, since approx. 1973, she was maintaining an escalating cocaine addiction, which peaked in approx. 1978-79 as a freebase addiction. She eventually went through a successful rehab process, and began working again in 1981 with a new development deal at MGM.

"I had started using cocaine and amphetamines way before the success. And in fact had had quite a bit of success working behind them.[...] There was a definite progression that went from using it as a tool, to using it as a crutch, and then you weren’t using it for anything except that it was life itself."

Her relationship with Spielberg was never the same, and the two went from close friends to cordial strangers in a matter of years, without as much as a conversation inbetween.

In 1991, Phillips, with the encouragement of her friend and fellow writer, author Anne Rice, finished her first novel, You’ll Never Eat Lunch in This Town Again, which went on to be a New York Times Bestseller, and to sustain a lingering cultural relevance.

"Steven" is one of the story’s main characters, who travels with the protagonist along the story’s central arc. On September 13, 1977, at the tail end of post production for Close Encounters, while renegotiating merchandizing rights with a beleagured studio, Phillips is banned from the Columbia lot, which quickly becomes a gossip column headline and an embarrassment to the production, before descending further into—and eventually escaping—her addiction, to write her own book about her own experiences.

"Don't get mad, get even," a career rival, Dan Melnick, had told her early on, after she'd piqued his professional interest in The Sting, only to leverage it to command a better deal with Universal. And Melnick had been there too, that day, September 13th, 1977.

#17. The four screenwriters of Jurassic Park (1990-1992)

Dr. Grant and Dr. Sattler are archaeologists on a dig, used to brushing fossils by hand. They’re introduced to a seismic camera that takes a ‘shot’ into the dirt, and then they crowd around a set of monitors to watch a computer render an image. The technician tells us that within a few years it’ll make digging unnecessary, to Grant’s dismay.

But he only realizes how radically the world is changing when Hammond arrives, who flies them off to his kitschy jungle resort on Isla Nublar (“Cloud Island”). Once there, Hammond displays his miraculous creative technology before taking them to a movie theater to show them how it’s done. The movie theater is also a theme-park ride that turns to reveal it’s adjacent to a genetics lab and a computerized control room.

And all of this reimagines the way the real life filmmakers encountered seamless CGI. One of Jurassic Park’s defining quirks is the way it mythologizes its own unprecedented technological powers. We will see that this repetitive meta-reference functions as a new kind of surface distraction, obscuring deeper dynamics.

Jurassic Park’s dinosaurs were originally going to be achieved using a combination of animatronics, puppeteering, and stop-motion animated models, with CGI only to be used for subtle background work.

The form of stop-motion they were using was called Go-Motion. Tippett had developed it at George Lucas’ special effects company, Industrial Light and Magic, before starting his own company, Tippet Studio. Go-Motion worked by using a computer to synchronize a camera with motorized puppets, allowing for a motion blur effect that improved realism compared to traditional stop motion. ILM was going to help implement it.

But when ILM received the commission in June ‘91 a few of their engineers thought they should be in charge, most notably Steve Williams and Mark A.Z. Dippé, who had been directly responsible for the astonishing but conspicuous CGI work on The Abyss, and Terminator 2, which won ILM two Oscars. Williams and Dippé thought CGI was ready, finally, to become invisible. But Dennis Muren, ILM’s VFX Supervisor, wasn’t so sure. He also didn’t want to venture in the direction of poaching a high-profile contract from a friend and colleague.

Working in secret, Williams built a T-Rex skeleton from scratch—the same method he’d used for the T-1000. By November ‘91, he’d given it a walk cycle, in time for an anticipated visit by Amblin producers Kathleen Kennedy and Frank Marshall. He kept it playing on a monitor where they couldn’t help but notice it. To Kennedy, “it was immediately very clear that we were going to get realistic movement to these dinosaurs that was going to be far advanced from what we were doing with stop motion.” They commissioned further tests.

First, ILM delivered a demo of a herd of running gallimimus skeletons. Spielberg was astonished. The footage reminded him of National Geographic. “I looked at it and I couldn’t speak after watching it. I just couldn’t even catch my breath. I had never seen anything like it. Because even though it was just a skeletal frame of Gallimimus, this herd of Gallimimus was moving just as realistically as if we had taken a camera and filmed them for real.”

But he needed to see a T-Rex walking in broad daylight before he could be sure. The engineers at ILM made 3D scans of the dinosaur models to make matching digital versions. That highly anticipated test was screened for Spielberg, Kennedy, Marshall, Tippett, and Lucas, among others.

Everyone who was there to see it says they felt like they were witnessing the dawn of a new age. A computer generated entity passing for real.

As Lucas said:

"Everyone had tears in their eyes. It was like one of those moments in history, like the invention of the lightbulb or the first telephone call. A major gap had been crossed and things were never going to be the same."

As the story goes, Tippett declared himself “extinct,” although he was soon brought back into the fold as the dinosaur motion supervisor.

Spielberg took the frisson of this world-historic moment and fed it back into the story.

We can track this development in the changes that happened to the story between the first draft—developed with Michael Crichton and others through 1990 and 1991, before the decision to use CGI—and the final draft, developed with David Koepp through 1992.

In October ‘89, Spielberg was chatting with Michael Crichton in his office about another project, when he found out about Crichton’s latest book, Jurassic Park, which Crichton had just delivered to his publisher. Crichton had written a screenplay about a graduate student cloning a pterodactyl from fossil DNA in 1983, but required several years to work out the kinks. Spielberg asked to read it, and the next day the two agreed they wanted to make it together.

But in May 1990, as the book was getting ready for print, Crichton’s agents started a bidding war. Spielberg went to Universal Studios, to secure the funding, emphasizing the property’s franchising and theme park potential. They were especially interested in the T-Rex river chase.

In addition to $1.5 million, Crichton was paid $500,000 to write the first draft of the screenplay. Crichton said he was sick of the dinosaurs and the characters at that point, but Spielberg said they just needed someone who could pare it down fast. Crichton said he’d do it, with the provision that he’d eventually hand it off for someone else for the character polishes. He got started in July 1990, even before the book was released.

Crichton delivered his first draft seven weeks later, on September 7th, 1990. Although this draft has never been published, in 2022, archivist Derrick Davis summarized its contents. By Spielberg’s request, and Crichton’s intention, the first draft is a straightforward adaptation that “has all the big scenes retained and has the logical flow that appears in the much longer and more extended argument.”

It succeeded in establishing the general outline that would be used in the finished movie.

  • Once Grant and the kids return,
    • Tim and Lex are chased by raptors into the kitchen,
    • Ellie and Muldoon distract the raptors outside,
    • Someone resets the power
  • One of the kids gets the computers back on in the Control Room
  • There’s a showdown in the rotunda involving the T-Rex skeleton
  • Hammond meets his fate
  • They escape by helicopter

Spielberg wasn’t satisfied with it. He said that it “start[ed] too fast.” The next draft included a prologue, and pushed back the scene where the visitors first see the sauropods, delaying the gratification of seeing the "monsters," like in Jaws. Crichton turned in his final "chunk" on January 19th, 1991, and said he had taken his best shot.

The plot was still rough around the edges. But besides that, Crichton’s draft had flat characters, weak laughs, and lacked inspiration, failing to punctuate its emotional moments.

Spielberg began shooting on Hook on February 19th, 1991, which ran overschedule and budget, occupying his attention until June. Soon after starting, Malia Scotch Marmo, who had just successfully reworked the character of Captain Hook, was enlisted to work on Jurassic Park. She found out from her husband that Amblin had optioned Crichton’s novel; she got a copy and was reading it outside the studio one day, when Kennedy noticed, started a conversation, and invited Marmo to try a rewrite. Marmo and Spielberg talked a lot about the story while shooting the movie, but Marmo only officially started her draft in October, and only finished it on March 14th, 1992.

Although Marmo read Crichton’s draft, she decided to start fresh and work from the novel. She said her principal contribution was to make Sattler and the children more assertive. Crichton had prioritized scientific verisimilitude, as he had in the book, but Spielberg said that “effects are only as good as the audience’s feeling for the characters.”

Marmo also brought out the romantic comedy and “mating ritual” between Grant and Sattler, who’d already been married in Carter’s version. Jurassic Park gained much of its colour during her tenure. But overall her take was too juvenile. Spielberg said, simply, “I’ve read it twice, and I think it’s a miss.”

At this point, Koepp was brought on from Robert Zemeckis’ Death Becomes Her (1992), and quickly pulled the script into shape in time for the production’s August start date. Koepp said he never even read the previous drafts.

So goes the surface history of Jurassic Park. But a closer look, aided by other materials unearthed by Davis, including early storyboards, story memos, and a lost draft by Production Designer Rick Carter (Back to the Future Part II), suggest a far more continuous development largely governed by Spielberg himself.

Months before his first script meeting with Crichton in July ‘90, Spielberg was already working out the story. He’d pored through his copy of the book, highlighting his favourite passages and scribbling notes in the margins. Spielberg said, "What I wanted to do was boil the book down and choose my seven or eight favorite scenes and base the script around those. So we crunched the book.”

Carter was the first creative professional brought onto the project, within weeks of acquiring the property. They hired seven artists to help conceptualize the story: five primarily doing storyboards (Marty Kline, Ed Varreaux, Tom Cranham, Dave Lowry, John Bell) and two primarily doing paintings (Dave Negron, and Craig Mullens).

After they crunched the book and established its major setpieces, Spielberg started to dig in.

Carter observed that Spielberg begins by envisioning a physical layout: “once he knows what the space is—that's very important, to know where things are—he'll just start playing a projector in his head. He’s open to contributions, and he’ll talk it through, but he'll actually draw these funny little frames which are incredibly detailed if you know how to look at them.

Koepp would later say he found the storyboards “enormously helpful. It was like having a large portion of the movie just handed to you, to be able to walk around and soak up the feel of what the movie’s supposed to be like.”

Some of the recently published storyboards appear to have been produced prior to Crichton’s first draft.

In this frame, for example, we see an older Tim (11) and a younger Lex (7 or 8). These were their ages in the novel, and they were only reversed with Koepp’s draft, since Spielberg wanted Joseph Mazzelo as a younger Tim, and “realized that if I cast Lex younger than Tim, she would look about five years old—which was much too young to put in jeopardy with these dinosaurs. It would have turned the audience against us completely.”

But here Lex is drawn with dark hair, while in Crichton’s novel (earlier) and his drafts (later) she’s described as a blonde. She’s also holding a doll, rather than the baseball mitt has in Crichton’s novel and both of his drafts. No accessory is mentioned in Carter’s version, and by Marmo’s it’s a flashlight instead.

By Crichton’s first draft, the kids are described as being protected from the invading T-rex by a plastic bubble that “falls over them like a shield.”

Afterwards, the T-Rex lifts the car into her jaws, attempting to eat the children alive. Lex falls out of the car into the mud, still holding her doll. Unlike in the book, however, she lands just underfoot of the T-Rex.

Crichton’s first draft introduces more mutilation than exists in the corresponding scenes in the book. In the first draft, and only the first draft, The T. Rex is described as chomping down on Malcolm’s legs, and then pinning Ed Regis (a deleted tour guide character) into the mud with her claws. Then, according to Davis’ summary: “The rex grabs him in his jaws, and lifts him above the limbs of the tree. We don't see what happens. Body parts seem to fall, but it's hard to tell in darkness.” Then the gore showers onto Grant and the kids below.

In Crichton’s second draft, Nedry’s body is discovered ripped into parts.

In Marmo’s, the dismemberment is expressed as comic relief.

Even before finishing his first draft, Crichton was told to drop the Pterodactyl scene that had made the initial crunch. It was eventually used in Jurassic Park 3.

Finally, Crichton’s first draft contains a new ending. In the book, Hammond stumbles into a ravine, and is eaten alive by ‘compys’, small dinosaurs with narcotic venom.

In Crichton’s first draft, this happens instead in the rotunda, after the hanging dinosaur skeleton and scaffolding crashes down onto him, pinning him to the ground.

Crichton turned in his second draft on January 19th, ‘91. 3 days later, Spielberg, Carter, and Steve Starkey met to discuss the script with an eye to reducing the budget. And 6 weeks later, Carter turned in a draft of his own.

While each of Spielberg’s writers attests to his close involvement, his exact influence is obscured by their product. Carter’s draft—a “collage” of notes and storyboards that claims mostly to reflect the state of the story pre-Hook—suggests a more transparent view into Spielberg’s vision for Jurassic Park as it existed after Crichton and before Marmo.

Carter’s version is the only one bookended by helicopter scenes, beginning with the In-Gen helicopter, and ending with a military helicopter.

Carter’s opening adapts the prologue from the book, in which some of Hammond’s men deliver an injured worker to a jungle clinic. Ed Regis tells the doctor that the worker was injured in a construction accident and mentions a “backhoe” but she suspects that he was mauled. In a line not in the book, she conveys her doubt by remarking that “it looks like he was eaten by a bulldozer.” The patient moans “lo sa raptor” before dying and then Carter cuts from the bite marks to the raptor jaws Grant is uncovering. The association between industrial machines and dinosaurs is echoed later on in a description that says that a trail deforested by dinosaurs looks like a “giant bulldozer went berserk.”

Marmo’s draft introduces a similar analogy, this time between the helicopter and the dinosaurs, during the Nublar landing scene. (Marmo’s analogy is ambivalent—it’s difficult to entirely distinguish whether it refers to a T-Rex, a velociraptor, or some hybrid with a mosquito or a dragonfly. As a symbol, then, it reflects Freud’s principle of condensation.)

In Koepp’s first draft (the only other version to contain the helicopter opening)—as in the book—the doctor looks up the definition of “raptor” and learns that it means “bird of prey.” Then, in Koepp’s early versions of the dig scene, the ROAR of the arriving helicopter is described as sounding “like some giant beast descending on the camp.” But Koepp also retains the bulldozer; he opens with a newly imagined scene that shows the accident itself, when a bulldozer, which also ROARS, attempts to push a crate into a holding pen, and an attending worker is slashed by whatever’s inside.

In Crichton’s second draft, the lawyer that approaches from the helicopter is told that he’s standing on top of the skeleton. In Carter’s version, the helicopter lands “right on top of the dig site.” In Koepp’s early versions, the helicopter arrives at night. Grant “looks up at the helicopter and shouts, shaking his first.”

In Carter’s version (only), during the Nublar helicopter descent, when the pilot loses control, Ellie asks Hammond if they’re going to crash. In Crichton’s second draft (only), it’s Lex who asks. In Marmo’s version (only) the helicopter is twice described as shaking violently.

Carter’s draft closes by introducing the new “T-rex finale” that “came from Spielberg’s fertile mind” during the January 22nd ‘91 story meeting. In previous versions, the park had either been blown up by an army helicopter in a series of fiery explosions (Crichton’s first version) or destroyed by a genetically-engineered lysine deficiency akin to a plague (Crichton’s second).

The T-Rex leaps up and grabs the helicopter by the skids, causing the pilot to lose control.

This version lasts through Marmo’s draft (in which it becomes an “army helicopter”), but is gone by Koepp’s: “I was not privy to that one. Steven must have moved on from that one before I got there.”

On Marmo’s request, Spielberg lent her his personal copy of the galleys, with highlights and margin notes. She also stayed in touch with Crichton. Her version includes a description of a bearded Grant, and storyboards from this era feature Grant with glasses, an Asian Ellie (although Marmo is quoted as saying she pushed for Dern as Ellie), and the dark-haired Lex.

Marmo’s version introduces the electric fence as an ominous new obstacle, and the roadside wall-drop sequence.

But it’s only with Koepp’s version that Tim is electrocuted by the fence and stops breathing, before Grant resuscitates him.

By the time Marmo finished her script, Koepp had already been given his assignment and Spielberg’s full attention. He decided to start fresh from the novel rather than modifying earlier drafts. But he worked from the copious storyboards and ideas generated by the creative team in conjunction with the previous screenwriters and Spielberg. As well, during the writing, he received story notes from Marmo, and ongoing input from Spielberg, whom he found to have “wonderful suggestions for action scenes.”

Spielberg had emphasized to Marmo that each scene should be “weighty with narrative ingenuity,” and Koepp continued the process of compressing the story. He refined the movie’s pairs of opposites, such as chaos-order, nature-artifice, man-woman, parent-child, science-religion, carnivore-herbivore, creator-creation, etc.

The kids were kind of a challenge, because we didn’t want it to seem like they were there just to have kids in the movie. Steven, in particular, is vulnerable to criticism like that, because he likes to work with kids. He works with kids awfully well, so it’s not entirely fair, but the perception is there nonetheless. So our challenge was to find out why these kids were essential to the movie. (Koepp, qtd. in Shay 57)

Koepp turned in an early working draft sometime between April 23rd and May 1st, 1992; a draft on May 26th, of which only 10 pages have been released; an update on June 30th (full script); followed by numerous revisions during principal photography, with a Final Shooting Script dated November 11th and a Final Draft dated December 11th, 1992.

Koepp’s version, like all previous versions but Marmo’s, expands on a moment of criminal negligence from Crichton’s novel. The novel includes three separate dinosaur incidents on mainland Costa Rica: (1) the arrival by helicopter of a young (“no older than 18”) injured worker to a jungle clinic; (2) the attack of a young girl by compys (later used in The Lost World); and (3) a fatal attack on an infant by compys while a nanny is distracted. Eventually word of the strange lizards is funneled to Columbia University, where it’s redirected to Dr. Grant for his expert opinion. This coincides with the arrival of Grant’s research funder, Hammond, whom Grant has been informed has been buying up amber. Later in the movie, Grant spies baby velociraptors stowing away on the supply ship. Besides Crichton’s second draft, which includes the second incident (a scene discarded within days), the drafts focus on the first incident.

Koepp’s first draft drops the idea of the raptors already having made it to the mainland, but opens for the first time with the “bulldozer accident.” He then reintroduces Carter’s jungle clinic scene, including Crichton’s beat of the doctor learning that a “raptor” is a “bird of prey.” And he continues with a scene at an amber mine, first conceived in Marmo, but now with Hammond learning that the injured worker is suing them for $2 million dollars. In every subsequent version this is adjusted to $20 million. By the May version, Koepp and Spielberg realized that the jungle clinic beat was just a rehash of Jaws’ “this is not a boat accident.” They omitted it, but allowed the original accident to remain as a catalyst for the investors to pressure Hammond, who must then court Grant’s approval to smooth over the disturbance.

Koepp’s crucial tweak was making Dr. Grant hostile towards children, creating a dramatic tension that doesn’t exist in the novel or previous versions. Grant uses his prop claw (introduced in Crichton’s second draft) to slash at and terrorize a child—already re-enacting the opening scene. Critic Henry Sheehan finds this moment to be echoed again in the main road T-Rex attack, in which Grant interrupts his own barely "suppressed murder fantasy” by snapping out of a trancelike moment of indecision.

Koepp finally removed the raft sequence, which he found “rather redundant.” The Jurassic Park River Adventure at Universal Studios had been being developed since at least May 1990. Spielberg called it “a three-dimensional chance to live the movie,” although he admitted he would be stepping out before the steep drop at the end, being, himself, afraid of heights.

Koepp also continued to develop the ending. Each draft since Crichton’s first develops a scene involving the collapse of the T-Rex skeleton in the rotunda (not in the book). In Crichton’s first draft, we recall, it collapsed onto Hammond. In his second, it collapses as Grant climbs it, and serves as a distraction. In Carter’s, Tim and Lex try to escape the raptors by climbing into the T-Rex’s rib cage, to no avail; Grant arrives at the last minute, presses some control buttons, which causes the skeleton to collapse, with the T-Rex skull crushing one of the raptors. Marmo’s version is similar. Koepp’s refines the concept further, introducing a "Condor" crane that Grant maneuvers to knock down the skeleton, ripping it from the ceiling and sending cables and bolts down like bullets, and with the skull decapitating the raptor—beheading the raptor with a beheaded head. (cf. Aristotle, Poetics 13.1452a)

In the final draft (the change was only made on-set) the skeleton collapses on top of Tim—marking the third time that a chassis has done so, a conspicuous repetition.

Koepp also completes the rehabilitation of John Hammond. After Hammond was crushed in Crichton’s first draft, he distracts the raptors in the second, sacrificing himself in a final act of redemption to save the kids.

Carter’s version is similar, except that Hammond admits to knowing the DNA was unstable and that the park was never tenable. In Marmo, he admits it but blames it on his employees, and is left behind and dies. In Koepp’s first draft, he admits he knew, but blames it on himself, and stays behind but we don’t see him die. By Koepp’s June 30th ‘92 draft, the DNA isn’t inherently unstable, Hammond’s culpability for the park and bringing the kids there is unclear, and he is brought along on the helicopter. Koepp speaks to the softening of Hammond directly: “Steven identified with him, whether consciously or not.”

Marmo said, “I thought [Hammond] was insane because he made a park that had raptors and T. rexes in it and still he brought his grandchildren to it before it had a real safety check. He took his own grandchildren to a potentially lethal park to use them as pawns.” But, “Steven wanted a sunnier captain of the Jurassic ship; he was clear about that.”

By the final draft, the question of culpability for the story’s concrete acts of negligence—the young worker mauled by a raptor whose family is suing Hammond for 2 or 20 million dollars; the boy and girl repeatedly threatened with violent death—is overshadowed by Hammond’s idealism and the ultimate rescue of the children.

But Koepp’s versions also enact a more fundamental reframing. In the book, Dr. Wu compares DNA to photography: “The DNA of the dinosaurs was like old photographs that had been retouched, basically the same as the original but in some places repaired and clarified...” (137). The analogy begun in the textual form of Jurassic Park the book only comes to full reflexivity in Jurassic Park the movie which is, of course, a sequence of hundreds of thousands of retouched “old photographs.” Koepp’s draft marks the arrival of a meta-consciousness concerning not only the movie’s own character as a theme park attraction, but as a state of the art technology demonstration.

Reconstituting the components of the miracle at ILM, Koepp’s first draft restores the early brachiosaurus scene from Crichton’s first draft and sequences it next to the movie theatre/computer lab/genetic nursery, amalgamated into a single rotating theme park ride.

"I’d love to take credit for it, but I think it was Steven’s idea. It was brilliant. We could pop in for a few seconds here and there, put up some essential bits of information, and then get out. ... and it would make sense because it’s all part of the amusement park environment."

This conscious, ironic meta-reference became the finishing layer of Jurassic Park’s aesthetic.

Spielberg was dreaming himself into his movies—as he always did—but had intuited a strange new hook. It was a dream about discovering a new power for making dreams come true—just as, in reality, he was.


As a celebration of the historical turning point and their new ability to create seamless CGI, they showcased it.

Throughout the story, they remind the spectator of their newly harnessed powers for capturing the imagination. As critic Nigel Morris puts it:

Jurassic Park inflects knowledge, always present in spectatorship, that what is experienced is not real—though the experience is real and what is experienced may be realistic—by heightened awareness of the stimuli’s synthetic nature. (Empire of Light, Ch. 15)

This shifting was both cause and effect, a mesmerizing feedback loop that bounded the spectator’s sense of being both safely protected behind a screen and completely enveloped in the story.

This allowed a new transparent dimension that could be saturated with advertising that didn’t feel like advertising. By pretending it was criticizing itself, it disarmed the spectator, whose criticisms they heard addressed, and neutralized.

It obscured the apparent absolution fantasy of a grown man haunted by the death of two children in an unforeseeable industrial accident.

#18. The Fabelmans (2022) is About the Limits of Honesty

Spielberg’s most comprehensive biography to date is Joseph McBride’s Steven Spielberg: A Biography, first published in 1997. But it was “unauthorized.” Spielberg has always discouraged potential biographers, telling them that he was planning to write his own autobiography at some time in the future.

McBride notes Spielberg’s desire to “control the telling of his own life story.” He writes:

I am constantly surprised by how many people, including some in the literary world, react with automatic suspicion when they hear the phrase "unauthorized biography," as if there were something inherently dubious about a book not having the subject's seal of approval. On the contrary, what should arouse the reader's suspicions are the inevitable constraints placed on an author's integrity by the decision to allow his subject to authorize and thereby to control the writing of the book. By talking endlessly about his life in press and television interviews to promote his movies, Spielberg already has given us an autobiography of sorts, albeit a scattered, fragmentary, and sometimes misleading one; what largely has been missing from the picture is an independent examination of his character, seen not simply through his eyes alone but also through the perspectives of the people who have known and worked with him throughout his lifetime.

McBride details some of these “misleads” in his work. Spielberg has a habit of telling “fibs.” Most famously over the years, for example, he’s told various more-or-less elaborate versions of how he got his break at Universal Studios. The template is that he sneaks off a tour, hides in the bathroom, and then occupies an office and gradually insinuates himself into the studio. The truth (which is no less impressive) is that his father’s colleague contacted the Universal Studios librarian, Chuck Silvers, who got Spielberg a day-pass and offered to show him around. Spielberg made a strong impression on him, and Silvers offered him a summer internship, but couldn’t get him a pass, and so Spielberg had to find his own way onto the lot.*

It may seem trifling to address such imprecisions, but many of these fibs sustain a weird purchase in reality. In 1970, Spielberg subtracted a year from his age, an adjustment he maintained in the media and on government documents, like his driver’s licence, until 1995 (past the time of Jurassic Park) when it was only corrected as the result of a lawsuit. In 1975, his adjusted age allowed him to void an unfavourable contract on the basis that he’d signed it before he was 21. It was only after McBride sought out Spielberg’s birth certificate and published the history of the claims that the counter-party was able to settle their valid legal dispute.

Spielberg started making good on his promise of an autobiography as early as 2002, with Catch Me If You Can. Of course, as we have seen, Spielberg’s work generally has a strong autobiographical tendency. But Catch Me If You Can signalled a “more literal” reflection of his own family trauma. (McBride)

Despite his frequently told anecdote suggesting his lack of susceptibility to Freudian analysis, he also began to admit the opposite. In conversation with Scorsese about the film at the time of its release, Spielberg noted that:

The older I get, the more I go back to the early memories to get me to commit to a movie. It takes sometimes something very Freudian to get me to say, 'Well, this is interesting; I better read beyond page fifty.'"

In 2017, HBO produced Spielberg’s first authorized biography: Spielberg (2017). Spielberg’s mother, Leah Adler, encouraged him to turn it into a movie.

Spielberg has always been fascinated by coming-of-age stories, but in 2022, with The Fabelmans he finally turns the lens onto himself—still displacing his story onto fictional characters but to the minimal degree. And it’s precisely by undermining this apparent transparency that he begins to reveal the distortions that characterize his technique.

In an interview with director Paul Thomas Anderson, Spielberg once again describes his working relationship with screenwriter Tony Kushner as a sort of therapy session, acknowledging his first (but not his second) therapy experience.*

The Fabelmans begins like Bergman’s Scenes from a Marriage—a 1973 6-part TV series about the disintegration of a marriage—before finding its shape or “splitting” as Paul Thomas Anderson puts it.

Spielberg has previously used Scenes from a Marriage as a shorthand for the difference between him (and George Lucas) and Scorsese (and the rest of New Hollywood). One style he says, is introspective, and “concentric” while the other is a “complete thought” and “more linear.”

The story unfolds in the vein of Malick’s Tree of Life or Tarkovsky’s Mirror. Spielberg mythologizes his own biography as a genesis of myth-making, turning the particulars of his life—like having three younger sisters, being a Jew among Christians, and the child of an unstable marriage—into elements of a paradigmatic creation myth populated by the gods and goddesses of cinema.

The movie opens with one of Spielberg’s earliest memories: his mother and father taking him to the movies.

His parents are emblems of the Greatest Generation; his mother is a creative spirit playing the role of a housewife, and his father is an engineer laying the foundations of the personal computer.

When describing the memory elsewhere, Spielberg emphasizes two impressions: feeling overwhelmed and feeling deceived.

So the curtain is open and I expect to see the elephants and there's nothing but a flat piece of white cardboard, a canvas. And I look at the canvas and suddenly a movie comes on and it's The Greatest Show on Earth. At first I was so disappointed, I was angry at my father, he told me he was taking me to a circus and it's just this flat piece of color. [...] For a while I kept thinking, Gee, that's not fair, I wanted to see three-dimensional characters and all this was was flat shadows, flat surfaces. I was disappointed by everything after that. I didn't trust anybody. I never felt life was good enough, so I had to embellish it.

But in The Fabelmans this moment is played for awe. It’s superimposed over another, more primal memory, which he described to Time magazine in 1985: being wheeled down the aisle of a synagogue towards the red light of the Ark of the Torah, while elders on each side chanted prayers in rhythm and handed him crackers. And when asked in 1991 to choose a “master image” that recurs throughout his work, Spielberg chose that of Barry, the toddler from Close Encounters, approaching “that beautiful but awful light, just like fire.” Spielberg’s first memory is also his “master image,” and here he layers it under the later memory, rendering the cinematic image as the penultimate expression of the divine fire.

These two memories give way to a third: inspired by the train crash in the middle of The Greatest Show on Earth, Spielberg begins to crash his own trains together by flashlight.

His mother reflects on the artistic fulfillment she feels while playing Bach, especially when she descends into the lower octave of one of his laments, describing it as "making a world you can be safe and happy in,” and infers from this that crashing trains is her son’s way of gaining a sense of control over his life. His father isn’t conscious of it, but he takes just as much pleasure in tinkering with his machines.*

The subsequent scene plays like Freudian pornography (clip). And later, when the family goes camping just after he turns 16, his mother dances in a nightgown as the men of the family gaze on. His father’s best friend tells Sammy that he should be filming this, and turns on the headlights of the car behind her, making her gown transparent. Once again the illuminated moving image is infused with ambivalence. The embarrassed elder daughter tries to block her, but Sammy urges her to sit down, which the father doesn’t overrule.

Later, while editing the dancing footage on his father’s request, he notices evidence of his mother having an affair with the friend. This is the moment that gives the movie its “shape,” echoing the earlier “secret,” and splitting Sammy from the rest of his family.

Sammy and his mother, as co-conspirators, play out an emotional affair in parallel to her actual affair, which eventually results in the family’s divorce. Spielberg had been accused of making Disney movies about incest before, with The Colour Purple and Back to the Future, and now the veil is even further lifted.

As Jocasta says to Oedipus in Sophocles’ tragedy, “many a man has slept with his own mother in a dream.”

But even when the movie’s psychosexual elements are made explicit, they hardly register until they become so loud that they’re hard to ignore. Once young Steven reaches senior year in Saratoga, his power to control reality becomes activated, not only within the story, but conspicuously in its telling.

After being bullied by two blonde antisemites, Sammy later finds himself in a private, playful moment in the library with one of their girlfriends and her friend. When the girlfriend points out that he’s Jewish, he replies, in close-up, “I mean, yeah, since the day I was circumcised.” The friend becomes attracted to him, associating him with Jesus (who was also Jewish, and circumcised), and invites him over to her room, covered in posters of Jesus and Elvis, so that Sammy might let Jesus “come inside him,” the first of many such double entendres.

Eventually Sammy gets his revenge on the bullies by screening a movie of the senior beach day that he’s edited to show the smaller Aryan as a thieving loser, and the bigger one as an Olympic god, including a close-up of his lower body while running. The bewildered pair turn against each other, and after Sammy admits his exact motives are unclear even to himself, he promises never to tell of the humiliation, “unless I make a movie about it.”

The autobiography is too Freudian to take at face value. Spielberg is playing with us the same way he says he played with his first therapist. The meta-implication is that Spielberg is showing us what it means to have a power to manipulate reality to make it safer for himself. It was in Saratoga that he realized that his movie camera could be used not only as his vehicle to social acceptance but as a “defensive weapon.”

Spielberg’s making conspicuous of his power for shaping reality is his way of telling us that there are some things he still can’t talk about, even at the sunset of his career; his Kurosawa years....

#19. And, as a corollary, all artworks

  1. Steven Spielberg’s Jurassic Park (1993) recently celebrated its 30th anniversary. In the years since its release, its intellectual property has grown into a “cottage industry,” encompassing theme-park rides, toys, apparel, video games, product placement, and five sequels, with a new “Jurassic era” set to begin in 2025. While Spielberg had already directed other movies that were hugely profitable, some, like Jaws (1975), Close Encounters (1977), and E.T. (1982), weren’t “toyetic,” as Mattel’s CEO once observed about Star Wars back in 1977 (Columbia had eyes on buying Mattel, until Star Wars blew the market wide open); and the other, Indiana Jones, belonged to George Lucas. Jurassic Park was the first, and last, enduring merchandising empire that Spielberg could call his own.

    At the time, Jurassic Park represented a breakthrough in cinema technology, marking the first time that a blockbuster depended on CGI that looked and moved realistically. It became the herald for the coming digital revolution, which Spielberg talked about with a mixture of excitement, melancholy, and trepidation. (Empire Magazine “How Jurassic Park Became the Biggest Movie of all Time” August 1993.)

    The filmmakers integrated this energy into Jurassic Park’s brand identity. At the same time as Jurassic Park movies operate at the cutting edge of creative technology, their stories are about the perennial danger of cutting-edge creative technologies, adding a new meta-referential gloss to the classic technology-critical story archetype (e.g., Prometheus, Golem, Frankenstein, Godzilla).

    But Jurassic Park also had a more personal significance. By 1992, Spielberg was in his mid-40s and onto his second marriage, with Kate Capshaw, who had just converted to Judaism, renewing Spielberg’s faith in the process. They had one child each from their previous marriages, one adopted son, and two children of their own. But he was still facing a transition to adulthood: both in his upcoming responsibilities of running a major Hollywood studio, Dreamworks SKG; and in the seriousness required by Schindler’s List (1993), which was being treated by Sid Sheinberg, Spielberg’s mentor at Universal Studios, as Spielberg’s arrival into his own identity as a mature filmmaker. It was his cinematic bar mitzvah and an emotional breakthrough.

    According to biographer Joseph McBride, Spielberg’s movies of the late ‘80s and early ‘90s reflected the filmmaker working through several of his well-publicized neuroses: his resentments towards his father (Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade); his divorce to his first wife, Amy Irving (Always); and his own Peter Pan complex (Hook). It was around this time that Spielberg had been trying some psychotherapy.

    All my friends went to therapy and I thought that maybe I would learn something about myself, so I went for a year. But I can't say that I found the discoveries conclusive. Everything I learned about myself I knew already or I'd guessed for myself.

  2. An Unresolved Syllogism in Freud?

    The patient would lie down on a sofa, while Freud sat behind him. The patient agreed to “say whatever came into his head, while ceasing to give any conscious direction to his thoughts.” The purpose of free association was the same as that of its predecessor (therapeutic hypnosis): to isolate maladaptive tendencies in the patient’s unconscious mental processes and resolve them. But it had been modified for a very specific purpose: to isolate a subtle form of what Freud called “transference.”

    In 1894, Freud was an early-career medical scientist. Rather in the way that Aristotle makes an analogy between a bent stick—i.e. the displaced, faint, quivering reflection of a stick—for an image, Freud studied, not delusions, per se (the quivering reflections), but their underlying physical causes.

    One day, his friend, a medical doctor named Josef Breuer, told him about a woman he was treating who suffered from “clouded” mental states. Breuer had figured out—after she once fell into one of these states in his presence—that he could engage her while she was in it, and even talk through it so that her waking condition would improve.

    Freud speculated that these memories were ordinarily clouded because considering them directly would be so shameful by the patient’s own standards that some part of their mind was somehow, actively, keeping it at bay, unbeknownst to the patient. The patients felt guilt in the form of displaced anxiety. If they could just be brought back to the original incident, and urged through it “with a free expression of emotion,” the pent-up energy would be released (“catharsis”) and their condition resolved.

    But Freud suspected that some of his patients’ traumatic recountings were embellished. And one day, one of his female patients, as she was waking up from her trance, tried to kiss him. The same thing had happened to Breuer.

    Freud realized that he’d failed to account for a key variable. His patients were responding to his analysis in exaggeration of what was reasonable, which either took the form of intensified positive feelings (compliance, love), or of intensified negative ones (criticism, anger). Either way, this exaggerated “transference,” since it was woven into the therapeutic relationship, was difficult to detect, and even harder to prove to the patient. He realized that he’d only ever been part of his patients’ own distorted re-tellings. And indeed, although the patients had seemed to improve, over the short-term, their symptoms soon returned.

    Considering this problem, he reframed his method. Instead, he would ask his patients to talk about their dreams. To the contemporary skeptics, then (and there were many)—who believed that the so-called realm of the unconscious was nothing but random epiphenomena—this provided a perfectly random sample. It was nonsense material, given meaning only in the patient’s interpretation./p>

    But if they agreed with Freud’s speculation—that dreams must provide the closest interface between the waking world and the “clouded” self—then all the better.

    And now, when the inevitable moment of transference occurred, the practitioner would be better positioned to see it for what it was, and explain it using the patient’s own associative links, which could be traced all the way back to early childhood experiences.

    Freud, reflecting later, observed that, by way of logical corollary, he’d also discovered several other arts at the same time. If psychoanalysis was a science, he realized, for example, then so was dream analysis. In other words, both could trace an apparent, “manifest” narrative back to its underlying, evolutionary “motive.”

    Freud, by analyzing his own dreams, outlined the basic ways the dream’s real purpose concealed itself. It expressed itself through an ongoing narrative, with multiple people from life being “condensed” into singular dream figures, and the actual functions of the dream “displaced” onto seemingly minor details. It was then finished with a “deceptive secondary revision,” with the relatively conscious side of the mind doing a bit of extra polishing. It was only as the analyst paid attention to the patient’s associative patterns, rather than their interpretations, that the underlying story became visible.

    And if dreams were basically stories, you see, then this implied two more sciences: art criticism, and consumer psychology.

    Poetry, like dreams, could be decoded as the imaginary gratification of the artist’s unconscious wishes. The analyst just needed to factor out their conscious orientation towards an audience: the poetry was “calculated to arouse interest in other people and were able to evoke and to gratify the same unconscious wishes in them too.”

    But did Freud’s bilateral syllogism really hold together the way Freud said it did?

    Isn't the real story the underlying ahistorical assumption that the basic evolutionary drives are human-beings’ primary motivators? And—willfully or not—coming to see everything through that "lens"?

#01n. The Deal Memo

This agreement summarizes the principal terms negotiated between Warner Bros. and the production entities associated with Mr. Steven Spielberg and Mr. John Landis concerning the motion picture tentatively titled Twilight Zone: The Movie.

The parties initially proposed a “negative pickup” structure, wherein the production team would independently finance and produce the picture, delivering a finished negative to Warner Bros. in exchange for payment. This financing model—customarily involving the procurement of a bank loan secured against the studio's distribution commitment—was rejected by Warner Bros., which requested a production framework with oversight, citing the historical reputations of the principals involved. In particular, Mr. Landis’s high-spending practices and Mr. Spielberg’s recent string of heavily supervised and commercially successful releases (Raiders of the Lost Ark, Poltergeist, and E.T.) were noted as material considerations.

The final terms of agreement, as executed, are as follows:

  • Mr. John Landis, acting through Levitsky Productions, Inc., shall receive total compensation of one hundred fifty thousand dollars ($150,000 USD), allocated as follows:
    • Thirty thousand dollars ($30,000 USD) for writing services;
    • Thirty thousand dollars ($30,000 USD) for producing services;
    • Ninety thousand dollars ($90,000 USD) for directing services.
  • Mr. Spielberg and Mr. Landis shall be co-producers of the picture, with all rights and obligations associated thereto shared equally (50/50 split).
  • Each co-producer shall receive five percent (5%) of the gross receipts of the picture. Said percentage shall escalate to twelve and one-half percent (12.5%) of gross receipts once cumulative gross surpasses fifteen million dollars ($15,000,000 USD), and shall further escalate to fifteen percent (15%) following the break-even threshold as defined in studio accounting terms.
  • Despite the picture’s modest domestic box office performance (~$34,000,000 USD), gross participation entitlements are projected to yield approximately three and one-half million dollars ($3,500,000 USD) per co-producer, exclusive of international, home video, and ancillary revenues.
  • Mr. Landis shall retain final cut rights for his segment of the film, contingent upon completion within schedule and budget (defined as within ten percent [10%] of approved cost) and the receipt of a Motion Picture Association of America rating no more restrictive than PG.
  • Credit provisions shall read as follows: “John acknowledges that Steven may have a presentation credit.” Mr. Landis shall receive onscreen and advertising credits for writing, directing, and producing, with such credits to be equal in size and treatment to all similar credits, excluding only the presentation credit granted to Mr. Spielberg.
  • Mr. Frank Marshall shall receive a flat fee of one hundred thousand dollars ($100,000 USD), one-half percent (0.5%) of gross revenues post-break-even, and three percent (3%) of net profits. Mr. Richard Matheson and Mr. George Miller shall receive a separate percentage of net profits commensurate with services rendered, in addition to agreed-upon fees.

Note: All production expenditures for Twilight Zone: The Movie were administered through Twilight Zone Productions, Inc. For any budgetary disbursement exceeding five hundred dollars ($500 USD), dual authorization was required. Cheques were co-signed by (i) Disc Management Systems, Inc., the payroll company (typically Frank Marshall), and (ii) Warner Bros. payroll operations (typically Jim Henderling, studio payroll executive). #02n. A Steven Spielberg Production?

Although several initial news reports stated that the tragedy occurred on “a Steven Spielberg production” (or similar), within days, Spielberg’s name was removed from the story. When the movie was finally released, it had no “Presentation credit.”

Twilight Zone: The Movie (1983) was most likely originally going to be “a Steven Spielberg Production.” This is only significant as part of the broader argument that Spielberg had every likelihood of being invested in the film’s production.

The deal memo stipulates that “Steven may have a presentation credit,” and the initial highly-researched NTSB report stated that “The film, “Twilight Zone” is a STEVEN SPIELBERG PRODUCTION funded by Warner Bros. Inc., Burbank California” (2); the revised edition (following several petitions from the defense) does not include this specification; perhaps because Spielberg never claimed the credit.

By July 1982, Amblin was still in its fledgling stages, sorting out its branding and product lines for the rest of the decade.

Spielberg’s first production entity was Amblin’ Productions, Inc. (est. 1970), named after the short film he'd just produced at the time. Spielberg would continue to use this as his personal loan-out company through 1980. During this time he produced the Gale and Zemeckis movie, I Wanna Hold Your Hand (1978), as “Steven Spielberg Presents.” (He also produced Zemeckis’ Used Cars (1980) but without a presentation credit.)

After the founding of Amblin Entertainment, Inc. in 1980, Spielberg produced Continental Divide (1981) as “an AMBLIN Production,” but this was a one-off, with “Amblin Entertainment Presents” occurring only once or twice through to the 90s.

Poltergeist (1982) is “A Steven Spielberg Production.” E.T. (1982) is “A Steven Spielberg Film,” and Goonies (1984) is a “Steven Spielberg Presents.”

By the mid-eighties, Amblin had established a two-tier product line: “Steven Spielberg Presents,” (for works not directed by Spielberg) and “A Steven Spielberg Film” (for works directed by Spielberg).

As well, it’s possible Twilight Zone was initially conceived by Spielberg as a potential franchise.

Ted Ashley, who was Serling's agent at the time that the writer created The Twilight Zone, had graduated to the position of chairman of the board at Warner Bros. Following the success of Star Wars, Close Encounters of the Third Kind, and Alien in the late seventies, Ashley decided the time was right to create a big-screen fantasy built around the concept of The Twilight Zone. Star Trek, another TV show that had a fanatical cult following, was being turned into a whole series of feature films. Ashley imagined the same future for Twilight Zone. (F&G 64)

“Before the accident, everyone was talking about sequels, and that idea was forgotten.” (Carol Serling, qtd. in F&G, 172)


#03n. “8:00 or 8:30pm”

In Landis’s testimony (Feb. 1987), he reported that on or around Friday, July 9th, 1982, a second meeting took place at his offices on the Universal Studios lot. Present were Marshall, Landis himself, Folsey, and Allingham. He stated that the first meeting—approximately Tuesday, July 6th—had centered on Marshall’s question about whether it would be possible to obtain waivers for young children to work past 6:30 p.m., which Marshall acknowledged was the legal cut-off. The defendants say Marshall told them he would have his colleague, Kathleen Kennedy, call the Labor Board to inquire.

At the second meeting, the defendants say Marshall informed them that waivers could only be obtained until “8:00 or 8:30 p.m” (James Neal’s opening statement; Landis’s testimony).

However, Colleen Logan, Area Administrator for the Division of Labor Standards Enforcement, testified that the Department’s policy was to grant extensions until exactly 10:00 p.m., when requested (trial testimony; cf. D'Agostino closing argument.)

If the defendants’ account of the “8:00 or 8:30 p.m.” restriction is accurate, then where did Marshall get that timeframe? Did he actually have Kennedy—or anyone—call the Labor Board?

If not, why not?

Is it possible that Marshall, given his experience as a producer, already knew waivers were unlikely to be granted—not because of the late hour, but due to the children's proximity to dangerous practical effects? If so, any call to the Labor Board would have drawn unwanted attention to the production and been clearly ill-advised. But then, why would Marshall present the issue to the defendants as one of scheduling rather than safety?

The point is that if the defendants were genuinely naïve or deferential to Marshall’s expertise, he may have misrepresented the obstacle as a matter of timing rather than danger—thus downplaying to them the seriousness of the subsequent illegal hiring, given his own acknowledged expertise on the matter of children, labour law, and filmmaking, as demonstrated by his 10+ years in the film industry and his work on Poltergeist.

#04n. A semantic misunderstanding?

Chain claims the existence of a series of interdepartmental Warner Bros memos detailing interviews with key figures involved in the production on the Warner Bros side in the days after the accident, including Lucy Fisher and Frank Marshall.

If the authenticity of these memos is accepted, it means that Lucy Fisher told Warner Bros. lawyers that she had specifically asked both Landis and Marshall whether the children newly added to the script would be “a problem,” and both men assured her that not it would not. She let them know if asked, she would deny any knowledge of the matter. (Chain Ch. 18; Ch. 47)

Marshall, for his part, allegedly told Warner Bros. lawyers that he had no idea what Folsey Jr.’s “Personal Cash advance” cheque was for, having assumed it was going to be used for its stated purposes. In his recounting of an early July meeting at Landis’s Universal Studios compound (he remembered just one, not two), he had told Landis, Folsey, and Allingham that it would not be possible to use young children later than 8:30. Instead, he suggested, they should pursue waivers to hire 13-year-olds that look young, which he assumed they did for the shots in which Marshall saw the children prior to the 2:20am shot.

Marshall said that for that shot in particular, he was under the impression that dummies were going to be used. This was because at that same meeting, when he told Allingham to “forget about” the possibility of using children legally, Allingham said it wouldn’t be a problem because it would be a "long shot." What Marshall could have understood by that was "we will be far away so we can use dummies without the spectator being aware,” but what Allingham meant was, “we can put Morrow and the kids further away from the explosions than it will appear on the screen so it will be safer than it looks on film.”

The devil is in the details, however, and the question remains why Marshall, with his experience, thought it was appropriate even for 13-year-olds to be in the conditions he witnessed Myca and Renee to be in?

And why didn't he double check, given such flimsy assurances by untrustworthy agents, of a nefarious act reported by a reliable 3rd party over a month in advance of Landis doing exactly that?

#07n. “Fly by Night”

Allingham presented himself to prospective helicopter pilot Clay Wright as working for “Fly By Night” (Chain Ch. 4). According to Chain, “Fly by Night Productions” was the name that Landis had given his segment’s production company for Twilight Zone, in original contract negotiations with Warner, before the deal became structured so that no production entity specific to Landis was needed. According to LaBrecque, at least, it was a “joking tribute to Steven Spielberg, who had called his own production company by that nickname” (26). Chain points out, as well, its utility in telegraphing a thrifty indie film rather than a studio-backed major. “Fly by Night” was painted on the nose of the helicopter; its existence is noted by Budds in his Oct. 26 supplementary police report: “The helicopter was olive-drab in color with a painted decal on the nose which read, "Fly by night.”” The decal was erased by an unknown person in the later morning hours of July 23rd, 1982. #08n. Steve Williams

Steve Williams resists the emphasis on Tippett’s “extinction” narrative. Williams had been Animation Supervisor on The Abyss and Terminator 2, and now, despite single-handedly redeeming the prospect of seamless CGI for Jurassic Park, that role was given to Tippett. “Kathleen Kennedy pats me on the back and goes, ‘You have a very bright future.’ And that was that. That was the first and only time I ever met her. I never saw her again.” He was astonished by the lack of recognition that followed his breakthrough, failing to be acknowledged even in Jurassic Park’s Best Visual Effects Oscar speech.


#09n. Steve Starkey

Steven Starkey had been an editor at ILM assigned to edit a promotional documentary for Frank Marshall: The Making of Raiders of the Lost Ark. He would go on to edit The Making of Poltergeist and The Making of Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom, before moving into the Amblin bungalow on the Universal lot and was encouraged to pivot to Associate Producer by Frank Marshall, who was solidified as his “mentor.” Starkey was hired on to Jurassic Park in September 1990, when he was put in charge of preparing a budget and a production plan in coordination with Carter. They had previously worked together on Amazing Stories and Back to the Future Part II and III. Their first budget was too expensive for Spielberg, so they began to make script revisions. Eventually, Steve Starkey turned these into a 3-page document that allowed Spielberg to greenlight the budget. But once Hook started, Starkey was put onto other projects (he’d initially felt “jilted” when his contribution had been minimized, but was quickly offered the role of Producer on Death Becomes Her). Carter and Starkey’s work would eventually become Carter’s draft, following a parallel evolution from Crichton’s first draft; but then incorporating the changes Spielberg liked from Crichton’s second along with ideas brainstormed a few days later. Carter knew Crichton personally and had been in touch with him to keep apprised of story developments. He’d also submitted several story memos of his own. Although Tom Stoppard (Empire of the Sun, 1989) was being considered, Carter knew Marmo was also in the conversation but was concerned that she was going to (or did, eventually) take the story down “the wrong path.” The existence of Carter’s draft was only uncovered in 2020 by Derrick Davis. He turned it into an illustrated audio drama, imagining Carter’s version brought to life, in which he combined the script and story memos with his collection of rare and unreleased concept art and storyboards, including an entire binder that had belonged to Tippett.
