WHY IJPAUC?

Suppose that Steven Spielberg’s mid-career magnum opus, Jurassic Park (1993), reflects the filmmaker's own guilt over his involvement in the illegal hiring of two children killed on a “Steven Spielberg Production” in 1982. Even if true, why would this discovery matter, all these years later, in today’s far more cynical times?

WJC April 24th, 2026 VVIP26

In my seminal media essay, IJPAUC? (2025), I argue that Jurassic Park (1993) is an unconscious confession. By that I mean that: when Spielberg’s emphasis on the pertinence of psycho-analyis to his work is taken seriously, the evidence is overwhelming that the franchise-founding ‘90s blockbuster bears heavy imprints of the infamous 1982 Warner Bros’ Twilight Zone: The Movie helicopter accident that took the lives of two immigrant children knowingly hired by the production as unpermitted “extras,” Myca Le (7), and Renee Chen (6).

I am not the first to make the connection between Jurassic Park (1993) and the Twilight Zone accident; I am, however, the first to ask whether it constitutes a “confession.” In other words, I consider the cultural status of Jurassic Park in light of the unanswered—and indeed, unasked—question of whether Spielberg in any way (tacitly or explicitly) sanctioned the illegal hiring. This question is motivated by the facts that Spielberg's Amblin Entertainment Co-Founder, Frank Marshall, is deeply implicated in the illegal hiring, and avoided two subpoenas (one international) for his testimony in 5 years abroad. As well, virtually all of the segment's production materials went missing. At the time, Spielberg was notorious for his prolific "thumbnail sketches," and for being a back-seat director (e.g. on Tobe Hooper's Poltergeist, 1982).

In the three books written about the case (Special Effects, 1987; Outrageous Conduct, 1987; Fly by Night, 2022), each by reporters who sat in on the trial, the question of Spielberg’s involvement in the planning of the fatal sequence remains one of plausible and serious interest. In short, the lion’s share of the blame for the tragedy fell upon co-producer/writer/director John Landis: he along with two production subordinates were charged with two counts of manslaughter, one for each child. But they maintained that co-producer Spielberg’s camp—the more experienced group of filmmakers, who had been tasked by the studio with keeping an eye on Landis)—were well-aware of the illegal hiring and had given it their blessing. On close analysis, the facts of the matter largely support the Landis group's claim. As for Amblin, they've let the films do the talking. Most recently, Frank Marshall produced Jurassic World: Rebirth (2025).

The purpose of this supplementary essay is to explain the significance, or upshot, of my conclusions as to Spielberg’s involvement based on the available evidence. I’ll begin by recapping the accident and the argument for Spielberg’s involvement in its particulars, before turning to what I see as the moral and poetic fallout of that involvement, given Spielberg’s titanic status as an American poet.

In the end, do our narratives carry any real moral currency? And if so, upon what is that currency based?

  1. The Twilight Zone Accident (July 23rd, 1982) (tap to expand)

    At 2:20am, the two children are carried across a river—Le under one arm, Chen under the other—as the anti-hero, played by 53-year-old veteran Hollywood actor Vic Morrow (also killed), tries to deliver them to the safety of the opposite shore. But the fourth and fifth explosions fired blow through the roof of a fishing hut and destroy the tail rotor of the helicopter, causing it to spiral downwards onto the spot where Morrow had just moments earlier stumbled to his knees. Renee had for a few brief seconds slipped from his grasp; plunged face-first into the murky waters below.

    Thanks to the diligent work of singular investigators in the NTSB and the Los Angeles D.A.’s office, charges of Involuntary Manslaughter were brought against five members of the film production, headlined by John Landis, the movie’s co-producer, and the writer/director of the segment involving the children.

    Landis, along with two subordinates (his Associate Producer and Production Manager), admitted to soliciting the children without permits (a misdemeanour with a maximum $5,500 fine) so as to allow the production to work the children at night for the sake of artistic effect, i.e., for the sake of realism, which was entirely consistent with how they'd gone about making The Blues Brothers (1980). They denied that they saw any special danger in this despite the practical effects and pyrotechnics called for by the shot, because safety would be in the hands of trained professionals; the state challenged this assertion.

    The charge of Involuntary Manslaughter is commonly misunderstood. The state accepts that the defendant(s) did not intend the accident or the deaths. Since every defendant would maintain that the accident, being accidental, was beyond their control, the state must prove that it would have been foreseeable to a reasonable person in their same situation. In any case, however, what is being faulted is not the deaths per se, but the defendant's character, as reflected by the state they had allowed themselves to be in at the time of their reckless actions pertaining to the accident.

    After a long and contentious string of legal proceedings including a full criminal trial (1986-1987), the five defendants were finally acquitted in 1987. A $200-million dollar lawsuit filed by the families in the days after the accident was settled for approx. $2-million dollars per family during closing arguments.

    What does any of this have to do with Spielberg, who was only 36-years-old at the time, and preoccupied with the release and reception of E.T.: The Extra-Terrestrial (July 1982)?

  2. Evidence of Amblin's Involvement (June/July 1982) (tap to expand)

    Twilight Zone: The Movie was an anthology film co-produced by Landis and Spielberg. It featured four different segments by four different directors, of which Landis’s—an original, written by Landis, eventually titled simply ‘Time Out’—was the first to go to production.

    Spielberg was among 14 defendants named in the civil case. But the suit had been filed in the early days after the accident and cast a wide swath. Not only Spielberg, but Joe Dante and George Miller—the directors of the other two segments—were named, who plainly had nothing to do with the hiring or accident. In the wake of the accident, Spielberg distanced himself from Landis who was previously a friend or friendly rival, and by popular telling acquitted himself admirably of whatever trivial association existed between himself and Landis.

    The question is: did Spielberg in any way know in advance that Landis intended to hire two children without permits in order to be able to film on location at night in proximity to industrial machine and pyrotechnics?

    And would this not explain Marshall’s otherwise bewildering tolerance of Landis’s flagrant employment of minors in proximity to obvious illegality and danger? And Spielberg’s continued association with Marshall, not to mention two other Amblin’ contractors also deeply implicated in the illegal hiring?

    Landis and his two “office” co-defendants—who were on the hook for two counts of Involuntary Manslaughter for the hiring alone—maintained that Executive Producer Frank Marshall gave the explicit permission to pursue the illegal hiring.

    Marshall of course being one third of Amblin, Entertainment, Inc., along with Spielberg, and Kathleen Kennedy. The defendants said that Marshall said that Kennedy had called the labor board to see whether it would be possible to get waivers allowing the children to work at night legally.

    Marshall denied having anything of significance to say. D.A. Gilbert Garcetti, however, made clear that the state made: “every effort possible to subpoena Frank Marshall but he was able to get out of our hands.” Kennedy’s never had reason to speak on the matter. Both Marshall and Kennedy were stopped on their way into Spain in early 1987, following an order by Interpol; but by that time their testimony was no longer of any use to the state.

    Spielberg’s given a few highly-curated public-facing statements, but nothing more specific than, “In response to your request, I was never at the Indian Dunes location of Twilight Zone on the night of the accident or at any other time. I declare under penalty of perjury that the foregoing is true and correct, executed at Los Angeles, California, this first day of December, 1982“ directed at an NTSB request for clarification after a truck driver told them that, on the night of the accident, Spielberg was on set and had asked him to procure him a car. (The driver later said he must have mistaken Marshall for Spielberg.)

    Whatever suspicion Landis's narrative might incur, Landis et al are as biased witnesses as we can imagine in this situation, hypothetically.

    So what do the facts of the matter tell us?

    Marshall’s signature was on the cheque used to hire the children for the first day of filming (with a dispensation of cash from the Beverly Hills Bank of Los Angeles, care of Landis’s mother, Shirley Landis, a teller at the bank, whose initials appear on the back of the cheque). A production assistant told the police that “the day of the accident or the day before,” Marshall’s accountant, working out of Amblin’s offices, handed her an envelope, which the PA later observed to be full of $100-dollar bills.

    Marshall has several layers of legal accountability, which superficially sufficiently motivates his reluctance to testify.

    But as a consequence no one has ever been able to ask Spielberg or either of his aides, whether he knew about the illegal hiring. Landis and his production regulars meanwhile were situated on the Universal Studios lot.

    Of course, all three were on an incredible creative and box office run, that had started with Raiders of the Lost Ark, and continued through Poltergeist and E.T., with Twilight Zone: The Movie, the next on the way. It would be understandable if all three had been totally preoccupied at that time (March-July 1982). But fan magazines at least represent Spielberg hard at work on his own segment, a sprawling, suburban, Halloween nights’ tale, in which a vicious neighbourhood bully receives his comeuppance, with makeup/costume work by the legendary make-up artist Rick Baker (who would go on to do Thriller the following year). Miller has said that he first met with Marshall and Kennedy in May of 1983 when they tapped him to direct a segment; Dante has said that the original concept for Twilight Zone: The Movie was that the plot would be far more continuous than the final edit became.

    As Landis’s attorney (Harland Braun) wrote, in protest, to the Los Angeles D.A.’s office, in November 1985, when the case was finally on its way to a full-blown, media-frenzied, triple-homicide trial, with billions of dollars in liabilities at stake in the pending $200-million dollar civil suit filed by the families. Landis could very well lose, and go to state prison, for his role in the accident. If this alibi was a bluff or a fib, it would be no laughing matter. And yet Braun wrote:

    With respect to Mr. Spielberg, Marshall and Kennedy will be able to state whether he knew that children were to be employed without permits. It could be reasonably inferred that given the close relationship between Spielberg, Marshall and Kennedy and Spielberg’s close supervision of his own movies, that it would be almost inconceivable that Marshall and Kennedy would not tell Spielberg that children were to be employed without permits on Spielberg’s own movie.

    In other words, if you’re not going to turn over these rocks and ask these questions, you can’t blame our clients for thinking what they were doing wasn’t that bad, relative to standards which hadn’t seemed to bother a few "acknowledged industry experts". The defendants had a plausible, unchallenged, exonerating narrative, at least for the illegal hiring! (The letter prompted the state’s second, international subpoena for Marshall’s testimony, but again it was of no use.

    The lawyer additionally observed:

    In addition, the letter that was obtained from Spielberg after the accident is interesting in that he only states that he was not on the set the night of the accident but nowhere addresses himself to the issue of whether he knew the children were employed without permits. Since that employment was an issue at the time the letter was written, one would assume that he could have denied his guilt simply by adding an additional line.

    At the time, Spielberg had developed a reputation for being professionally overbearing. He was contemporaneously (June 1983) involved in a public relations battle over his supposed micro-management of Tobe Hooper’s Poltergeist set (in 1981). (Rather like the way Lucas hovered over Spielberg’s shoulder on Raiders; just making sure he got it right.) He’d just been fined by the DGA for a trailer which showed Spielberg’s name three or four times the sizes of Hoopers, on the end credits card of a trailer that it’s unlikely the studio would have released without Spielberg’s approval.

    Spielberg is well-documented by fan magazines and making-of documentaries (ironically often produced or directed by Frank Marshall) to have been a prolific storyboarder around this time; filling album after album with cartoon sketches, which would later be condensed by illustrators into production storyboards. However, neither the production’s storyboards for the segment, nor hardly any other production evidence was ever turned over to investigators. Marshall, of course, is documented by Landis’s secretary at the time as raiding their production offices for various documents and files, before evading authorities and staying abroad filming Amblin movies for 5 years.

    If Spielberg’s group was giving feedback to Landis’s group through the intermediary Warner Bros.’ Executive production apparatus, in cognizance of the directors’ personal relationships, and that feedback is reflected in either the (3rd) draft of Landis's script, in which the Vietnam segment is introduced, or the (4th) draft of Landis's script, which add a few subtle notes, namely the addition of a simple new prop, which ended up being held by the little girl in the final shot: a “broken and naked Barbie Doll.”

    If Landis’s motives are known to have been creative, then why couldn’t Spielberg’s?

    In 1985, during an interview for The Color Purple, CNN’s Sandy Kenyon asked Spielberg if he had any comment on the upcoming trial. Spielberg waved his hands and asked that the cameras be turned off.

    Lest the very suggestion be disregarded, Amblin, at the time, had itself an alarming history of child endangerment, including incidents on Jaws (1975), and E.T. (1982), along with several examples of carelessness and brutality towards stuntpersons, 1975-1982.

  3. The Basis of Hope in Trust

In Aristotle’s Rhetoric, he argues that those who write about the science of persuasion neglect the role of the speaker’s perceived personal character in its function.

...for it is not in the way some of the speech-art makers assume in their “arts,” that the decency of the speaker contributes nothing to his persuasiveness, but character, one might say, has in it just about the most decisive means of persuasion. (I.2, trans. Sachs).

The “problem” of this project can finally be described in its full dimensions:

The question of assigning responsibility for the Twilight Zone tragedy is eclipsed by the screaming, bullying, maniac who'd been calling the shots that night: director John Landis, already a major figure in American film history.

Yet someone else, orders of magnitude more significant to the cinema arts is also implicated; or, at least, has been shielded from the question by those who were (like Executive Producer Frank Marshall).

Why have we so far been unable to ask or even “see” this question? And what does it mean or matter?
